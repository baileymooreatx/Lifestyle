# Move!

Get up to walk around at least once per hour. The consequences of sitting 
for long periods of time are detailed here.

## Cardiovascular Risks

Prolonged sitting significantly increases the risk of **cardiovascular disease**, 
including heart attack and stroke. Studies show that sitting for more than 6 to 
8 hours a day is associated with a **125% to 150% increase in cardiovascular 
events**. One landmark study found men who watched over 23 hours of television 
weekly had a **64% higher risk of dying from cardiovascular disease** compared 
to those who watched 11 hours or less. Experts suggest inactive individuals 
face a **147% higher risk** of heart attack or stroke. Reduced blood flow during 
sitting leads to blood pooling in the legs, increased vascular resistance, and 
elevated blood pressure—factors contributing to clot formation and endothelial 
dysfunction.  
  
## Metabolic and Endocrine Effects  
  
Extended sitting disrupts the body’s ability to regulate insulin and glucose.
After just one day of excessive sitting, **insulin sensitivity declines**, 
prompting the pancreas to produce more insulin, which may lead to type 2 
diabetes. Research indicates that people who sit for long periods have a **112% 
higher risk of diabetes**. For every two-hour increment in sitting time, 
diabetes risk increases by **7%**. Sedentary behavior also raises the risk of 
**metabolic syndrome**, characterized by high blood sugar, elevated blood 
pressure, excess abdominal fat, and unhealthy cholesterol levels—even among 
those who exercise regularly.  
  
## Musculoskeletal Damage
  
Sitting for long durations weakens major muscle groups and alters posture. The 
**gluteal and leg muscles** weaken from disuse—a phenomenon sometimes called 
"gluteal amnesia"—reducing stability and increasing fall risk. Prolonged 
sitting shortens **hip flexors** and tightens **hamstrings**, contributing to 
**lower back pain** and poor spinal alignment. Poor posture while sitting, such 
as slouching or craning the neck forward, stresses the spine, shoulders, and 
hips, potentially leading to chronic pain and joint degeneration. Reduced 
movement also slows circulation, increasing the risk of **varicose veins** and 
**deep-vein thrombosis**.  
  
## Cancer Risk  
  
Extended sedentary behavior is linked to a higher risk of several cancers,
including **colorectal, endometrial, lung, ovarian, and prostate cancer**. The 
exact mechanisms are not fully understood, but theories suggest that excess 
insulin production from inactivity may promote cell growth, while reduced 
physical activity lowers antioxidant defenses that neutralize cancer-causing 
free radicals. One analysis found that those sitting over eight hours daily with 
no physical activity face a **cancer mortality risk comparable to smoking and 
obesity**.  
  
## Mental Health Impact  
  
Sedentary lifestyles are associated with **higher rates of anxiety and 
depression**. Reduced physical movement limits the release of endorphins and 
other mood-regulating neurotransmitters. Additionally, remote work and reduced 
social interaction—often accompanying prolonged sitting—further impact 
emotional well-being. Studies also link excessive sitting to **increased stress 
at work** and overall lower life satisfaction.  
  
## Cognitive Function  
  
Prolonged sitting negatively affects brain health. Research shows that 
**uninterrupted sitting for two hours reduces cerebral blood flow**, impairing 
memory, attention span, and processing speed. Movement stimulates blood 
circulation to the brain, supporting clearer thinking and focus. Sedentary 
behavior has been associated with a higher risk of **dementia** and long-term 
cognitive decline, emphasizing the importance of regular physical breaks for 
mental performance.  
  
## Strategies to Mitigate Risks  
  
The negative effects of sitting are not irreversible. Experts recommend 
**breaking up sitting time every 30 to 60 minutes** with at least **five minutes 
of light activity**—such as standing, stretching, or walking. Simple changes 
like **walking phone calls**, using **standing desks**, taking the stairs, or 
parking farther away can significantly reduce risks. The concept of 
**nonexercise activity thermogenesis (NEAT)**—small daily movements like 
fidgeting or pacing—helps maintain metabolic activity. While 150 minutes of 
weekly moderate exercise is beneficial, it does not fully offset prolonged 
sitting; **frequent micro-movements throughout the day are essential**.
