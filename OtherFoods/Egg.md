# Eggs

Eggs are widely considered a **nutritional powerhouse**, offering a unique
combination of high-quality protein, essential vitamins, minerals, and healthy
fats in a low-calorie package. They are one of the few foods that contain almost
every nutrient the human body needs.

## Key Vitamins and Minerals

Eggs are rich in micronutrients, with the yolk containing the vast majority of
vitamins and minerals.

* **Choline:** A single egg provides roughly **147 mg** (about 27% of the daily
  adequate intake), which is crucial for brain health and cell membrane
  function.
* **B Vitamins:** Eggs are packed with B vitamins, particularly **vitamin B12**,
  riboflavin (B2), and folate. One egg offers about **0.45 mcg**
  (19% of the Daily Value) of vitamin B12 which is essential for nerve
  function, energy production, and red blood cell formation.
* **Vitamin D:** Eggs are one of the few natural food sources of vitamin D,
  which is vital for bone health, calcium absorption, and immune system
  function.
* **Selenium:** A powerful antioxidant, with one egg providing **15.4 mcg** (28%
  of the Daily Value).
* **Other Nutrients:** Eggs also provide significant amounts of **vitamin
  A**, **selenium** (a powerful antioxidant), iodine, phosphorus, and zinc.

## Nutrient Distribution: Yolk vs. White

The nutritional value is split distinctly between the two parts of the egg.

* **Egg White:** Composed mostly of water and protein (3.6g), the white contains
  virtually no fat, cholesterol, or carbohydrates. It provides about 17
  calories.
* **Egg Yolk:** Contains all the fat, cholesterol, and nearly all the vitamins
  and minerals, along with 2.7 grams of protein. It accounts for about 55 of the
  egg's 72 calories.

## Nutritional Profile

A single **large chicken egg** (approximately 50 grams) provides a dense
nutritional profile with about **72 calories**. It is composed primarily of
high-quality protein and healthy fats, with negligible carbohydrates.

The macronutrient distribution in one large egg supports muscle maintenance and
sustained energy.

* **Protein:** Contains **6.3 grams** of complete protein, providing all nine
  essential amino acids required by the human body. Approximately 3.6 grams are
  found in the white, while 2.7 grams are in the yolk.
* **Fat:** Provides **4.8 grams** of total fat, almost exclusively located in
  the yolk. This includes 1.6 grams of saturated fat, 1.8 grams of
  monounsaturated fat, and 1.0 gram of polyunsaturated fat.
* **Carbohydrates:** Eggs are very low in carbs, containing only **0.4 grams**
  per egg, with less than 0.2 grams of sugar and no fiber.
* **Cholesterol:** One large egg contains **186 mg** of dietary cholesterol,
  which is found entirely in the yolk.

## Cooking Method

Cooking eggs significantly alters their nutritional profile, primarily by
**increasing protein absorption** while potentially **reducing heat-sensitive
vitamins** and **increasing calorie/fat content** depending on the method used.

### Protein Bioavailability

The most significant nutritional change caused by cooking is the dramatic
increase in protein digestibility.

* **Absorption Rates:** The human body absorbs approximately **90% of the
  protein** in cooked eggs, compared to only **50%** in raw eggs.
* **Mechanism:** Heat denatures the protein structures, unfolding them so
  digestive enzymes can break them down more efficiently.
* **Biotin Absorption:** Raw egg whites contain **avidin**, an anti-nutrient
  that binds to biotin (Vitamin B7) and prevents its absorption. Cooking
  deactivates avidin, allowing the body to utilize the biotin present in the
  yolk.

### Vitamin and Antioxidant Retention

Heat exposure and cooking duration directly impact vitamin levels, with shorter
cooking times generally preserving more nutrients.

* **Heat-Sensitive Vitamins:** Prolonged high heat (such as baking for 40
  minutes) can reduce **vitamin D** content by up to **61%**, whereas shorter
  methods like boiling or frying reduce it by only about **18%**. Water-soluble
  vitamins like **B12** and **folate** can leach into cooking water during
  boiling, especially in hard-boiled eggs.
* **Antioxidants:** Common cooking methods reduce antioxidant levels (such as
  lutein and zeaxanthin) by **6–18%**. Soft-boiled or poached eggs retain more
  of these compounds than hard-boiled or overcooked eggs due to less heat
  exposure.
* **Fat-Soluble Vitamins:** Interestingly, some studies suggest that frying may
  slightly increase the measurable abundance of **vitamins A and E** compared to
  baking, likely due to the interaction with cooking fats, though boiling
  preserves them well due to the protective shell.

### Calorie and Fat Changes

The calorie count of an egg changes based on water loss and added ingredients.

* **Water Loss:** Cooking evaporates water, slightly concentrating the nutrients
  and calories per gram of weight, though the total calorie count of the egg
  itself remains similar (e.g., a large raw egg is ~72 calories, while a
  hard-boiled one is ~78 calories due to water loss concentration).
* **Added Fats:** The most significant caloric increase comes from the cooking
  medium. Frying an egg in oil or butter can increase the calorie count from ~72
  to **90–120 calories**, adding saturated or polyunsaturated fats depending on
  the oil used. Poaching and boiling add **zero calories**.

### Cholesterol Oxidation

The method of cooking affects the chemical state of cholesterol in the yolk.

* **Oxidized Cholesterol:** High-heat methods like frying, especially at high
  temperatures or for long durations, promote the formation of **cholesterol
  oxidation products (COPs)**. These oxidized forms are considered more harmful
  to heart health than native cholesterol.
* **Safer Methods:** Boiling and poaching involve lower temperatures (capped at
  100°C/212°F) and less exposure to oxygen, resulting in significantly **lower
  levels of oxidized cholesterol** compared to frying or scrambling at high
  heat.

## Complete Protein and Muscle Health

Eggs are a complete protein source, meaning they contain all nine essential
amino acids that the body cannot produce on its own.

* **Muscle Repair and Growth:** A single large egg provides approximately **6 to
  7 grams of high-quality protein**. This protein is highly digestible and has a
  superior biological value compared to many other protein sources, making it
  essential for building and repairing muscle tissue, skin, and organs.
* **Satiety:** The high protein content helps increase feelings of fullness by
  reducing levels of ghrelin (the hunger hormone), which can aid in weight
  management by preventing unnecessary snacking.

## Eye Health and Antioxidants

The yolks contain high levels of specific antioxidants that protect vision.

* **Lutein and Zeaxanthin:** These carotenoids accumulate in the retina of the
  eye and act as a shield against harmful light. Studies show they significantly
  reduce the risk of **cataracts** and **age-related macular degeneration**, the
  leading cause of blindness in the elderly.
* **Absorption:** The antioxidants in eggs are more effectively absorbed by the
  body compared to those from plant sources due to the presence of dietary fat
  in the yolk.

## Heart Health and Cholesterol

While eggs were historically criticized for their cholesterol content, modern
research has shifted this perspective.

* **HDL Cholesterol:** Eating eggs has been shown to raise levels of **HDL
  (high-density lipoprotein)**, known as "good" cholesterol, which helps remove
  other forms of cholesterol from the bloodstream.
* **Heart Disease Risk:** For the majority of healthy people, moderate egg
  consumption (up to seven eggs per week) does not increase the risk of heart
  disease. The nutrient profile, including omega-3 fatty acids (in enriched
  eggs) and choline, may actually support heart health by lowering triglycerides
  and reducing inflammation.
* **Preparation Matters:** The health impact can depend on preparation; boiling
  or poaching is healthier than frying in saturated fats or serving with
  processed meats like bacon.

## Weight Management

Eggs are an effective tool for maintaining a healthy weight due to their high
nutrient-to-calorie ratio.

* **Low Calorie:** One large egg contains only about **70 to 80 calories**.
* **Metabolic Support:** The combination of protein and healthy fats stabilizes
  blood sugar and insulin responses, providing sustained energy and reducing
  cravings throughout the day.

## Organic vs. Pasture-Raised vs. Conventional

Organic eggs offer a moderately different nutritional profile compared to
conventional eggs, characterized primarily by **higher levels of specific
vitamins and healthy fats**, though core macronutrients like protein and
cholesterol remain largely similar.

### Key Nutritional Differences

The most significant advantages of organic eggs lie in their micronutrient
density and fatty acid composition, driven by the hens' organic feed and outdoor
access.

* **Omega-3 Fatty Acids:** Organic eggs typically contain **2 to 3 times more
  omega-3s** than conventional eggs (approx. 0.13g vs. 0.05g per egg). This is
  due to the hens' diet often including flaxseed, algae, or natural forage.
  However, they still contain less than "omega-3 enriched" eggs, which can have
  up to 0.4g.
* **Vitamins A and E:** Studies consistently show organic eggs have **higher
  concentrations of vitamin A and vitamin E**. These fat-soluble vitamins act as
  antioxidants and are elevated because organic hens often consume more
  carotenoid-rich plants and insects.
* **Saturated Fat:** Some analyses indicate organic eggs may have **lower
  saturated fat** content (approx. 20% less) compared to conventional eggs,
  contributing to a healthier lipid profile.
* **Antioxidants:** Organic eggs often contain higher levels of **lutein and
  beta-carotene**, which are linked to eye health and immune function. This is
  frequently visually apparent in the **deeper orange color** of the yolk.

### Similarities and Misconceptions

Despite the differences in micronutrients, the fundamental building blocks of
the egg remain consistent across production methods.

* **Protein:** There is **no significant difference** in the amount or quality
  of protein. Both organic and conventional eggs provide approximately **6
  grams** of complete protein per large egg.
* **Cholesterol:** Contrary to some beliefs, organic eggs generally contain
  **similar amounts of cholesterol** (approx. 186 mg per large egg) as
  conventional eggs. While some pasture-raised studies suggest slightly lower
  levels, "organic" certification alone does not guarantee reduced cholesterol.
* **Minerals:** Levels of minerals like selenium, phosphorus, iron, and zinc are
  **comparable** between organic and conventional eggs, though trace mineral
  content can vary based on soil quality in pasture systems.

### The "Pasture-Raised" Distinction

It is important to distinguish between "organic" and "pasture-raised," as the
latter often yields even greater nutritional benefits.

* **Vitamin D:** While organic hens must have outdoor access, **pasture-raised**
  hens (which often overlap with organic) spend significantly more time outside.
  Exposure to sunlight allows hens to synthesize **vitamin D**, resulting in
  eggs with **3 to 6 times more vitamin D** than conventional or standard
  organic eggs.
* **Dietary Variety:** Pasture-raised hens supplement their feed with insects
  and worms, further boosting the **omega-3** and **Vitamin B12** content beyond
  what is found in standard organic eggs fed primarily on organic grain.

## Storage

To preserve nutrients and ensure safety, eggs should be stored **in their
original carton on a middle or bottom shelf** of the refrigerator, rather than
in the door or at room temperature.

The physical placement of eggs within the refrigerator significantly impacts
their quality and nutrient retention.

* **Avoid the Door:** Storing eggs in the refrigerator door is discouraged
  because it is the **warmest area** with the most **temperature fluctuations**
  due to frequent opening. These fluctuations can degrade protein quality and
  accelerate spoilage.
* **Middle/Bottom Shelf:** The main body of the fridge, particularly the middle
  or bottom shelves, provides a **consistent cold environment** (below 40°F /
  4°C). This stability is crucial for maintaining the integrity of
  heat-sensitive vitamins and preventing bacterial growth.
* **Keep the Carton:** Eggs should remain in their **original cardboard
  carton**. The carton protects the porous shells from absorbing strong odors
  from other foods (like onions or fish) and prevents moisture loss, which can
  concentrate nutrients unnaturally and degrade texture.

### Temperature and Nutrient Stability

Temperature control is the primary factor in preserving specific vitamins,
particularly vitamin D.

* **Vitamin D Retention:** Recent research indicates that storing eggs at
  **ambient temperature** (on the counter) rather than refrigerated may actually
  help retain higher levels of **vitamin D** in enriched eggs over short
  periods. However, for standard commercial eggs (especially in the US where
  they are washed), refrigeration is mandatory for safety to prevent
  *Salmonella*, which outweighs the marginal vitamin gain for most consumers.
* **Protein and Fats:** Consistent refrigeration prevents the oxidation of fats
  in the yolk and maintains the bioavailability of proteins. Fluctuating
  temperatures can cause condensation on the shell, facilitating bacterial entry
  that compromises the egg's nutritional value.

### Duration for Freshness

Proper storage extends the window during which eggs retain their peak
nutritional profile.

* **Refrigerated:** When stored correctly in the carton on a shelf, raw eggs
  maintain optimal quality and nutrient density for **3 to 5 weeks** beyond the
  pack date.
* **Room Temperature:** In regions where eggs are unwashed (common in Europe),
  they can last 1 to 3 weeks on the counter, but nutrient degradation
  (particularly of water-soluble vitamins) occurs faster than in refrigeration.
  In the US, washed eggs should not be left out for more than **2 hours**.
* **Signs of Degradation:** As eggs age, the yolk membrane weakens and the white
  thins, but the primary nutritional loss over time is a reduction in **vitamin
  A** and **riboflavin** levels.

## Float Test

An egg that floats in water is generally **old** and likely **bad**, but the
test primarily indicates **age** rather than definitive spoilage.

### The Science

The buoyancy of an egg is determined by the size of its internal **air cell**.

* **Fresh Eggs:** Sink to the bottom and lie flat on their side. They have a
  very small air cell and high density.
* **Older Eggs:** Stand upright on the bottom or tilt. As the egg ages, moisture
  evaporates through the porous shell and is replaced by air, enlarging the air
  cell. These are often still **safe to eat** but are better suited for
  hard-boiling (as they peel easier).
* **Floating Eggs:** Rise to the surface. This indicates the air cell has become
  large enough to make the egg buoyant. While this confirms the egg is **very
  old**, it does not automatically mean it is toxic.

### Safety vs. Freshness

It is crucial to distinguish between an egg that is **stale** and one that is 
**spoiled**.

* **Not a Guarantee of Spoilage:** A floating egg is definitely past its prime,
  but it may still be safe to consume if it has been refrigerated consistently
  and the shell is intact. The float test measures **density**, not bacterial
  presence.
* **The Sniff Test is Final:** The only reliable way to confirm if a floating
  egg is bad is to **crack it open** into a separate bowl.
* **Bad:** Emits a distinct, unpleasant **sulfuric or rotten odor**. The white
  may be pink, green, or iridescent, and the yolk may be flat and break easily.
* **Safe:** Has no off-odor and looks normal. If it passes the sniff test, it is
  safe to eat, though the texture may be watery.

### Recommendation

If an egg floats, **do not use it raw** or for dishes where texture is
critical (like poaching or frying). If it passes the sniff test, it is best used
in **baking** or **hard-boiled** recipes where texture differences are less
noticeable. If there is any doubt or a foul smell, discard it immediately to
avoid the risk of *Salmonella* or food poisoning.
