# Plant-Based, Non-Nutritive Sweeteners

Plant-based, non-nutritive sweeteners (NNS) are derived from natural sources and
provide sweetness with minimal to zero calories. The most commonly studied
include **stevia**, **monk fruit extract**, **allulose**, and **yacón syrup**.
These sweeteners are increasingly used as sugar alternatives, especially among
individuals managing diabetes or seeking metabolic health benefits.

## Insulin and Glucose Response

### Stevia

- **Source**: Extracted from the leaves of *Stevia rebaudiana*.
- **Insulin Response**: Most clinical studies show **no significant increase**
  in insulin or blood glucose levels after stevia consumption.
- Some research suggests stevia may **enhance insulin sensitivity** and even *
  *stimulate insulin production** in certain contexts, particularly in
  individuals with type 2 diabetes.
- A 2017 comparative study found that **Canderel Green Stevia®** did not
  significantly raise insulin levels compared to water in healthy adults.
- However, a 2022 microbiome study indicated that stevia might **alter gut
  microbiota**, potentially leading to **insulin resistance** in some
  individuals over time.

### Monk Fruit Extract (MFE)

- **Source**: Derived from *Siraitia grosvenorii*, a small melon native to Asia.
- **Insulin Response**: Clinical trials show **no impact** on blood glucose or
  insulin levels.
- In a crossover study with 10 participants, MFE caused **no rise in blood
  sugar**, unlike sucrose, which increased it by 70%.
- Animal studies suggest MFE may **improve insulin sensitivity** and help manage
  hyperglycemia and hyperlipidemia.

### Allulose

- **Source**: A rare sugar found naturally in small quantities in fruits like
  figs and raisins.
- **Insulin Response**: Allulose is **not metabolized** significantly and does 
  **not raise blood glucose or insulin** in healthy individuals.
- In people with diabetes or prediabetes, substituting allulose for sugar may 
  **reduce glucose impact by nearly 10%**.
- It may also **stimulate GLP-1**, a hormone that enhances insulin secretion and
  promotes satiety.

### Yacón Syrup

- **Source**: Extracted from the root of the yacón plant *Smallanthus
  sonchifolius*, native to South America.
- **Insulin Response**: Contains fructooligosaccharides (FOS), which are 
  **indigestible prebiotics**.
- One study showed that yacón syrup consumed with breakfast led to **lower
  glucose and insulin levels** compared to placebo.
- Its benefits are likely due to **prebiotic effects** that support gut health
  and metabolic regulation.

## Sugar Alcohols  

Unlike the non-nutritive sweeteners previously discussed, **erythritol** and
**xylitol** are **sugar alcohols** (polyols). While they are plant-derived and
lower in calories than sugar, they differ significantly in their metabolic
processing and insulin response.

### Erythritol

- **Metabolic Pathway**: Approximately 90% of ingested erythritol is absorbed
  into the bloodstream but **excreted unchanged in urine** within 24 hours. It
  is not metabolized by the body.
- **Insulin & Glucose Response**: Clinical studies consistently show erythritol
  has a **glycemic index of 0** and an **insulin index near 0**. It does not
  raise blood glucose or insulin levels in healthy individuals or those with
  type 2 diabetes.
- **Potential Benefits**: Some trials suggest it may **improve insulin
  sensitivity** and reduce HbA1c levels over time. It also stimulates gut
  hormones like **GLP-1**, which can blunt post-meal glucose spikes when
  consumed with carbohydrates.
- **Safety Note**: While metabolically neutral, recent observational studies
  (2023–2025) have noted associations between very high circulating levels of
  erythritol and increased cardiovascular risk, though causality remains under
  investigation.

### Xylitol

- **Metabolic Pathway**: Xylitol is **partially absorbed** and metabolized.
  Unlike erythritol, a portion is fermented in the large intestine, and some
  enters metabolic pathways that can influence glucose.
- **Insulin & Glucose Response**: Xylitol has a **low glycemic index (7–13)**
  compared to sugar (65–70). While it causes a **minimal rise** in blood glucose
  and insulin compared to sucrose, recent systematic reviews indicate it **does
  elevate insulin and glucose levels relative to placebo** (no intervention),
  unlike erythritol.
- **Potential Benefits**: Animal studies suggest xylitol may improve **glucose
  tolerance**, **insulin secretion**, and lipid profiles more effectively than
  erythritol in diabetic models. It is also well-known for preventing dental
  caries.
- **Toxicity Warning**: Xylitol is **highly toxic to dogs**, causing rapid,
  life-threatening hypoglycemia and liver failure due to a massive insulin
  release in canines.

### Key Distinction

While both are plant-derived, **erythritol** behaves more like the non-nutritive
sweeteners (stevia, monk fruit) with **virtually no insulin response**, making
it ideal for strict glycemic control. **Xylitol**, while far better than sugar,
does trigger a **small metabolic response** and contains calories, placing it in
a middle ground between sugar and zero-calorie sweeteners.

## Considerations

- **Individual Variability**: Responses to sweeteners can vary based on gut
  microbiome composition and metabolic health.
- **Long-Term Effects**: While short-term studies are promising, long-term
  impacts on insulin resistance and metabolic health require further research.
- **Regulatory Status**: All listed sweeteners have **GRAS (Generally Recognized
  As Safe)** status from the FDA.
