# Edible Plant Classification

The botanical distinctions between plant categories rely on **botanical 
structure** (which part of the plant is eaten) versus **culinary usage** 
(how it tastes and is prepared). The primary difference lies in the plant's 
reproductive function.

## Fruits

Botanically, a **fruit** is the mature ovary of a flowering plant, developed
from a fertilized flower to protect and disperse seeds. Any edible structure
containing seeds that grows from a flower is a fruit.

* **Examples:** [Apples](./Fruits/Apple.md), berries, and melons (culinary
  fruits); also [tomatoes](./Vegetables/Tomato.md), cucumbers, 
  [peppers](./Vegetables/ChiliPepper.md), pumpkins, and eggplants (botanical 
  fruits often treated as vegetables).
* **Key Trait:** Presence of seeds and origin from a flower.

## Vegetables  

**Vegetable** is a **culinary term**, not a strict botanical classification. It
refers to any other edible part of a plant that does not fit the botanical
definition of a fruit.

* **Roots:** Carrots, beets, radishes  
* **Stems:** Celery, asparagus, rhubarb  
* **Leaves:** [Spinach](./Vegetables/Spinach.md), lettuce, kale  
* **Flowers:** Broccoli, cauliflower  
* **Tubers/Bulbs:** Potatoes, [onions](./Vegetables/Onion.md),
  [garlic](./Vegetables/Garlic.md)  

## Legumes

**Legumes** occupy a unique intersection between botanical and culinary
categories.

* **Botanical Definition:** Legumes are plants in the family **Fabaceae** (or
  Leguminosae). The term also refers to the specific type of fruit these plants
  produce: a **pod** (a dry fruit that splits along two seams) containing seeds.
* **Culinary Usage:** While the pod is botanically a fruit, the seeds inside (
  beans, peas, lentils, chickpeas) are often cooked and eaten as **vegetables**
  or protein sources.
* **Unique Trait:** Legumes have a symbiotic relationship with nitrogen-fixing
  bacteria in their root nodules, enriching the soil.

## Herbs vs. Spices

Both herbs and spices are used for flavoring, aroma, or preservation, but they
differ by the **plant part** used and the **plant type**.

**Herbs**

* **Source:** Derived from the **leafy, green parts** of plants.
* **Plant Type:** Typically come from **herbaceous** (non-woody) plants, often
  grown in temperate climates.
* **Usage:** Can be used fresh or dried; generally have a milder flavor and are
  added late in cooking.
* **Examples:** Basil, parsley, cilantro (leaves), thyme, rosemary, mint.
* *Note:* Some plants provide both; e.g., cilantro leaves are an herb, while the
  seeds (coriander) are a spice.

**Spices**

* **Source:** Derived from **non-leafy parts**: seeds, bark, roots, rhizomes,
  fruits, flowers, or buds.
* **Plant Type:** Often come from **woody** plants, trees, or shrubs, typically
  native to tropical climates.
* **Usage:** Almost always used **dried** (whole or ground); possess potent,
  concentrated flavors and are often added early in cooking.
* **Examples:** Cinnamon (bark), ginger (root), cumin (seed), cloves (flower
  buds), saffron (stigma), black pepper (dried fruit).

## Detailed Breakdown  

### Fruits  

In botany, a **fruit** is the reproductive structure of flowering plants that
develops from the ovary after pollination and contains seeds. This definition
includes many foods commonly thought of as vegetables, such as tomatoes,
cucumbers, eggplants, and peppers, because they develop from the flower and hold
seeds. Culinary definitions, however, classify fruits based on flavor profile (
sweet/tart) and usage (desserts), excluding savory botanical fruits like squash
or okra.  

### Vegetables

The term **vegetable** has no strict botanical definition; it is a culinary
category encompassing any edible part of a plant that is not a fruit or seed.
This includes roots (carrots, beets), stems (celery, asparagus), leaves
(lettuce, spinach), tubers (potatoes), and flowers (broccoli, cauliflower).
Because "vegetable" is a broad cultural term, it often serves as an umbrella
category that can include legumes in dietary guidelines, even though legumes are
botanically distinct.  

### Legumes  

**Legumes** belong to the plant family Fabaceae (or Leguminosae). They are
unique because they produce seeds inside pods and have the ability to fix
atmospheric nitrogen into the soil through symbiotic bacteria in their roots.  

* **Botanical Status:** The pod itself is technically a type of dry fruit, and
  the seeds inside are the legumes.  
* **Dietary Status:** Due to their high protein content, organizations like the
  USDA often classify legumes (specifically dried beans, lentils, and peas,
  known as **pulses**) in the **Protein Foods Group**, though they can also
  count toward vegetable intake. Fresh legumes like green beans and peas are
  typically categorized as vegetables.  

### Berries  

Berries **are** fruits, but the answer depends on whether you are using
a culinary or botanical definition.

#### Culinary vs. Botanical Classification  

In **culinary terms**, berries are defined as small, sweet, juicy fruits used in
desserts and snacks. Under this definition, common examples include
strawberries, raspberries, blueberries, and blackberries.  

In **botanical terms**, a berry is a specific type of simple fruit produced from
a **single flower with one ovary**, where the entire outer wall ripens into a
fleshy pericarp with seeds embedded inside.  

* **True Botanical Berries:** Surprisingly, this category includes bananas,
  grapes, [tomatoes](./Vegetables/Tomato.md), kiwis, 
  [avocados](./Vegetables/Avocado.md), and 
  [blueberries](./Fruits/Blueberry.md).  
* **Not True Berries:** Common culinary berries like 
  [strawberries](./Fruits/Strawberry.md), raspberries,
  and blackberries are **not** botanical berries. They are classified as
  **aggregate fruits** because they develop from a single flower with *multiple*
  ovaries. Additionally, the "seeds" on a [strawberry](./Fruits/Strawberry.md)  
  are actually individual fruits called achenes, and the red flesh is a swollen
  receptacle, not the ovary wall.

## Key Overlaps and Exceptions

* **Tomatoes and Peppers:** Botanically fruits, but culinarily vegetables.
* **Green Beans and Peas:** Botanically legumes (and the pod is a fruit), but
  culinarily treated as vegetables when eaten fresh.
* **Peanuts:** Botanically legumes (growing underground in pods), not nuts,
  despite their name and culinary use as nuts.
* **Mushrooms:** Neither fruits, vegetables, nor legumes; they are fungi, though
  treated as vegetables in cooking.

## Nuts vs. Seeds

The primary difference lies in their botanical classification. A **nut** is a
specific type of hard-shelled fruit that does not open to release its seed,
whereas a **seed** is the embryonic plant itself capable of growing into a new
organism. In culinary terms, the distinction is looser, with "nuts" referring to
large, oily kernels with hard shells (often technically seeds or drupes) and
"seeds" referring to smaller edible kernels from flowers, vegetables, or 
grasses. While all true nuts contain seeds, not all seeds are nuts.  

### Botanical Definitions

Botanically, the distinction is structural and functional. A **true nut** is
defined as a hard-shelled, indehiscent fruit, meaning the shell does not split
open naturally to release the seed when ripe. True nuts develop from a compound
ovary and typically contain one or two seeds fused to the shell wall. Examples
of true botanical nuts include [hazelnuts](./Nuts/Hazelnut.md), chestnuts, and 
acorns.  

In contrast, a **seed** is the mature, fertilized ovule of a plant. It consists
of three main parts: the embryo (which develops into the new plant), the
endosperm (stored food), and the protective seed coat. Seeds are the
reproductive units found inside fruits, including nuts, drupes, and pods. While
a nut is a fruit type, a seed is the propagative part contained within it.  

### Culinary Classifications

In the kitchen and grocery store, definitions diverge significantly from botany.
The culinary term "nut" is a broad category for any large, oily kernel found
within a hard shell, regardless of its botanical origin. Consequently, many
foods called "nuts" are actually seeds or drupes.

* **Drupes:** Many popular "nuts" are technically the seeds of drupes (fleshy
  fruits with a hard inner stone). This includes [almonds](./Nuts/Almond.md), 
  cashews, [walnuts](./Nuts/Walnut.md), [pecans](./Nuts/Pecan.md), and 
  [pistachios](./Nuts/Pistachio.md). In these cases, the fleshy outer fruit is
  discarded, and the inner seed is consumed.  
* **Legumes:** Peanuts are technically legumes (seeds that grow in pods
  underground), not nuts, though they share a similar nutritional profile and
  culinary use.  
* **Culinary Seeds:** These are typically smaller and come from flowers (e.g.,
  [sunflower](./Seeds/Sunflower.md) seeds), vegetables (e.g., 
  [pumpkin seeds](./Seeds/Pumpkin.md)), or crops like [flax](./Seeds/Flaxseed.md) 
  and [hemp](./Seeds/HempHearts.md). They often lack the hard, woody outer shell 
  characteristic of culinary nuts, or the shell is easily removed.  

### Nutritional Profiles

While both are nutrient-dense, their profiles offer slightly different 
benefits.  

| Feature            | Nuts (Culinary)                                        | Seeds                                                                         |
|:-------------------|:-------------------------------------------------------|:------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
| **Calories & Fat** | Generally higher in calories and total fat             | Tend to be lower in calories and fat per ounce                                |
| **Protein**        | Often higher in protein (e.g., almonds, peanuts)       | Good source of protein, but often slightly lower than nuts                    |
| **Key Nutrients**  | Rich in Vitamin E, magnesium, and monounsaturated fats | Higher in fiber, zinc, iron, calcium, and specific antioxidants like selenium |
| **Usage**          | Often eaten as standalone snacks or in butters         | Frequently used as toppings, in baking, or blended into oils                  |

Nuts are particularly noted for heart-healthy monounsaturated fats and vitamin
E, which supports skin health. Seeds are often praised for their high fiber
content and mineral density, specifically zinc and magnesium, which support
immune function and hormone regulation.

### Common Examples

Understanding specific examples clarifies the confusion between the 
categories.  

* **True Nuts:** [Hazelnuts](./Nuts/Hazelnut.md), chestnuts, acorns, and beechnuts. 
  These meet both botanical and culinary definitions.
* **Seeds Called Nuts:** [Brazil nuts](./Nuts/BrazilNut.md) (seeds of a capsule
  fruit), pine nuts (seeds of pine cones), and coconuts (fibrous drupes).
* **Culinary Seeds:** [Chia seeds](./Seeds/Chia.md),
  [flaxseeds](./Seeds/Flaxseed.md), sesame seeds, poppy seeds, and
  [hemp seeds](./Seeds/HempHearts.md). These are strictly seeds and never
  classified as nuts botanically or culinarily.
* **Legumes:** Peanuts and soybeans. These are seeds of leguminous plants,
  distinct from tree nuts, which is a crucial distinction for allergy sufferers.
