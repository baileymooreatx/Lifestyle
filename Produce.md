# Produce

Which produce should you buy organic, rather than conventional?  

This information is based on the Environmental Working Group's 2026 
[Shopper's Guide to Pesticides in Produce](https://static.ewg.org/upload/pdf/EWG_SG-26_Guide-Print_C02.pdf).    
If budget is a limitation, focus on organic version of the Dirty Dozen.

## Organic vs. Conventional  

### Environmental Impact of Buying Organic  

Choosing organic supports broader ecological goals. Organic farming prohibits
the use of synthetic fertilizers and most synthetic pesticides, which leads to
several environmental advantages.

* **Soil Health:** Organic practices promote biodiversity and maintain healthier
  soil structures.
* **Water Quality:** By avoiding chemical runoff, organic farming reduces water
  pollution.
* **Biodiversity:** The absence of harsh chemicals protects pollinators like
  bees and other beneficial insects.

In contrast, conventional farming is associated with soil degradation and
potential harm to local ecosystems due to chemical leaching.

### Cost Considerations

The main drawback to buying organic is the cost. Organic bell peppers are
typically **significantly more expensive**, often 50% higher or more in
price than conventional ones. This premium reflects the higher labor costs,
lower yields, and more intensive management required for organic certification.
For budget-conscious shoppers, this price difference can be a barrier, leading
some to prioritize organic purchases only for items on the "Dirty Dozen" list
while buying conventional produce with lower pesticide risks (the "Clean
Fifteen").

## Produce Sticker Codes  

[Produce Sticker Codes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Price_look-up_code) 
(PLUs) are the stickers that appear on produce. They are 4 or 5-digit numbers
that identify bulk produce items at point-of-sale systems globally. They are 
used to identify produce, determine pricing, track inventory, and distinguish 
between conventional and organic produce.  
  
For example, 4011 is a conventional banana, while 94011 is an organic banana.

| Meaning                                            | Digits                        |
|:---------------------------------------------------|:------------------------------|
| Organic                                            | 5 digits starting with 9      |
| Conventionally grown, usually with pesticides      | 4 digits starting with 3 or 4 |
| Previously reserved for genetically modified (GMO) | 5 digits starting with 8      |

[Price Look-Up (PLU) codes](https://plufinder.com/) allows you to look up 
the PLU for produce.  

## Nontoxic Vegetable Wash  

I am specifically interested in [cleaning broccoli](https://www.wikihow.com/Clean-Broccoli) 
because it's not easy to clean like smooth vegetables are. For smooth 
vegetables, I usually use baking soda and a vegetable brush.  

### Baking Soda
1. Fill a bowl with water.
2. Add 1 teaspoon baking soda per 2 cups water.
3. Soak produce for 10 to 15 minutes.
4. Rinse thoroughly under running water.

👉 Research shows this helps break down some pesticide residues better than water alone

#### 🥬 For leafy greens (spinach, kale)
* Separate leaves.
* Soak and rinse multiple times.

#### 🍓 For berries
* Rinse gently under running water.
* Skip soaking too long because they get mushy.

### Hydrogen Peroxide  

This is taken from YouTube video 
[Hydrogen Peroxide](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=GKLZpquBc_4).
Hydrogen Peroxide kills bacteria.  

1. Mix 1/4 cup (2 fluid ounces) of 3% **Food Grade** hydrogen peroxide with a 
   gallon of cold water.  
2. Soak vegetables for 20 minutes.  
3. Rinse thoroughly under running water.  

### White Vinegar  

This is taken from YouTube video 
[White Vinegar](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=VmRtK4CVQ20).
White vinegar kills bacteria.  

1. Fill a large bowl with a mixture of 3 parts cold water and 1 part white 
   vinegar. Make sure the bowl is large enough to hold the broccoli, 
   and stir the mixture with a spoon to combine the water and vinegar.
   Include enough water so that the entire head of broccoli is completely 
   submerged. It may be useful to use a weight on top of the broccoli since it 
   floats.  
   For example, if you put 3 cups (710 mL) of water into the bowl, then 
   you would add 1 cup (237 mL) of white vinegar.
2. Place the broccoli in the solution to soak for 15 to 20 minutes.  
   Swirl the broccoli around the bowl a few times to loosen any larger debris, 
   and then let the vegetable sit undisturbed. While it’s soaking, you can 
   prepare other parts of your meal. 
3. Rinse thoroughly under running water.  

A vinegar bath takes slightly longer than a cold water bath, but can be 
more effective at removing pesticides and bacteria than water alone.
